Voyager Program

Humanity's Farthest and Longest-Lived Mission

Mission Overview

The Voyager program consists of two identical spacecraft, Voyager 1 and Voyager 2, launched in 1977 to take advantage of a favorable alignment of Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune. This "Grand Tour" configuration happens once every 175 years, allowing the spacecraft to use gravitational assists to visit multiple planets.

Originally designed to study just Jupiter and Saturn, the mission was extended to include Uranus and Neptune, and then further extended to explore the outer reaches of the solar system. Both spacecraft have now entered interstellar space, making them the most distant human-made objects in existence.

Key Facts

  • Launch Dates: Voyager 2: August 20, 1977; Voyager 1: September 5, 1977
  • Launch Vehicle: Titan IIIE-Centaur
  • Mass: 825.5 kg (1,820 lb) each
  • Power Source: Radioisotope Thermoelectric Generators (RTGs)
  • Current Speed: Voyager 1: ~38,000 mph; Voyager 2: ~34,000 mph
  • Current Distance: Voyager 1: 14+ billion miles; Voyager 2: 12+ billion miles from Earth
  • Mission Duration: 45+ years and counting
  • Communication Time: 21+ hours one-way

The Spacecraft

The Voyager spacecraft are identical in design, each equipped with:

Scientific Instruments

  • Imaging Science System (cameras)
  • Infrared Interferometer Spectrometer
  • Ultraviolet Spectrometer
  • Triaxial Fluxgate Magnetometer
  • Plasma Science Experiment
  • Low Energy Charged Particle Detector
  • Cosmic Ray Subsystem
  • Planetary Radio Astronomy Experiment
  • Photopolarimeter Subsystem
  • Plasma Wave Subsystem

Power and Communication

  • Power Source: Three Radioisotope Thermoelectric Generators (RTGs) using plutonium-238
  • Initial Power: 470 watts
  • Current Power: ~250 watts (decreasing by ~4 watts per year)
  • Communication: 3.7-meter high-gain antenna
  • Data Rate: Originally 115.2 kilobits per second at Jupiter, now reduced to 160 bits per second
  • Computer: Digital tape recorder and three interconnected computer systems

The Golden Record

Each Voyager spacecraft carries a gold-plated copper phonograph record containing sounds and images selected to portray the diversity of life and culture on Earth. The records are intended as a message to any extraterrestrial intelligence that might find them, and a symbolic statement for humans.

The contents of the record were selected by a committee chaired by Carl Sagan and include:

  • 115 images of Earth, life forms, and human activities
  • Greetings in 55 languages
  • Sounds of Earth (thunder, volcanoes, animals, etc.)
  • 90 minutes of music from different cultures and eras
  • Printed message from U.S. President Jimmy Carter

The record includes instructions for playback and a pulsar map showing Earth's location.

Key Discoveries

Jupiter

  • Discovery of Jupiter's faint ring system
  • Active volcanoes on Io (first active volcanoes observed beyond Earth)
  • Complex structures in Jupiter's atmosphere
  • Details of the Great Red Spot

Saturn

  • Complex structure of Saturn's rings, including thousands of ringlets
  • Discovery of several new moons
  • Detailed observations of Titan's atmosphere
  • Braided rings and "spokes" in the ring system

Uranus (Voyager 2 only)

  • Discovery of 10 new moons
  • Discovery of two new rings
  • Measurement of Uranus's rotation period
  • Observation of an unusual magnetic field tilted 60 degrees from the planet's axis

Neptune (Voyager 2 only)

  • Discovery of the Great Dark Spot (a storm similar to Jupiter's Great Red Spot)
  • Discovery of six new moons
  • Complete ring system with ring arcs
  • Observation of active geysers on Triton

Interstellar Space

  • First direct measurements of the interstellar medium
  • Data on the boundary between the solar system and interstellar space
  • Measurements of cosmic rays and magnetic fields beyond the heliosphere

Current Status

Both Voyager spacecraft continue to operate, sending valuable scientific data back to Earth despite being launched over 45 years ago:

  • Voyager 1: Currently in interstellar space, approximately 14.5 billion miles (23.3 billion kilometers) from Earth. It crossed the heliopause (the boundary of the solar system) in August 2012.
  • Voyager 2: Also in interstellar space, approximately 12 billion miles (19.3 billion kilometers) from Earth. It crossed the heliopause in November 2018.

Due to diminishing power from their RTGs, instruments are being turned off sequentially to conserve energy. The spacecraft are expected to continue transmitting data until approximately 2025, after which they will continue their silent journey through interstellar space.

Legacy

The Voyager program represents one of humanity's greatest achievements in space exploration:

  • Completed the first "Grand Tour" of the outer planets
  • Provided the first detailed images and data of the outer planets
  • First human-made objects to enter interstellar space
  • Demonstrated extraordinary engineering with spacecraft still functioning after 45+ years
  • Inspired generations of scientists, engineers, and the public
  • Carry humanity's message to the stars via the Golden Records

The data collected by the Voyager spacecraft continues to be analyzed and has informed numerous scientific papers and discoveries decades after the primary mission ended.
