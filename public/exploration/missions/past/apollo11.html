Apollo 11

The First Manned Mission to Land on the Moon

Mission Overview

Apollo 11 was the spaceflight that first landed humans on the Moon. Commander Neil Armstrong and lunar module pilot Buzz Aldrin formed the American crew that landed the Apollo Lunar Module Eagle on July 20, 1969, at 20:17 UTC. Armstrong became the first person to step onto the lunar surface six hours and 39 minutes later on July 21 at 02:56 UTC; Aldrin joined him 19 minutes later.

They spent about two and a quarter hours together outside the spacecraft, and collected 47.5 pounds (21.5 kg) of lunar material to bring back to Earth. Command module pilot Michael Collins flew the Command Module Columbia alone in lunar orbit while they were on the Moon's surface.

Key Facts

  • Launch Date: July 16, 1969
  • Landing Date: July 20, 1969
  • Return Date: July 24, 1969
  • Commander: Neil Armstrong
  • Lunar Module Pilot: Buzz Aldrin
  • Command Module Pilot: Michael Collins
  • Launch Vehicle: Saturn V
  • Famous Quote: "That's one small step for [a] man, one giant leap for mankind."

Achievements

Apollo 11 achieved its primary mission objective of performing a manned lunar landing and returning the mission safely to Earth. Among its many achievements:

  • First human landing on the Moon
  • First collection of lunar surface samples by humans
  • Deployment of the first scientific experiments on the lunar surface
  • Demonstration of the ability of humans to work on the lunar surface
  • Safe return of astronauts and lunar samples to Earth

Legacy

Apollo 11's successful mission to the Moon marked a significant achievement in human history and the culmination of the Space Race between the United States and the Soviet Union. It demonstrated the United States' technological prowess and fulfilled President John F. Kennedy's 1961 goal of "landing a man on the Moon and returning him safely to the Earth" before the end of the decade.

The mission's success paved the way for future Apollo missions and continued lunar exploration. It also inspired generations of scientists, engineers, and explorers to push the boundaries of human knowledge and exploration.
