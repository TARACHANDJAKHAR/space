Hubble Space Telescope

Revolutionizing Our View of the Universe

Mission Overview

The Hubble Space Telescope (HST) is one of NASA's Great Observatories and was launched into low Earth orbit in 1990. Named after astronomer Edwin Hubble, this space-based observatory has revolutionized astronomy by providing unprecedented deep and clear views of the universe, ranging from our own solar system to extremely remote galaxies forming shortly after the Big Bang.

Positioned above the distortion of Earth's atmosphere, Hubble has an unobstructed view of the universe, allowing it to capture images with substantially higher resolution than ground-based telescopes. It has become one of the most important instruments in the history of astronomy, with its observations helping resolve many long-standing problems in astrophysics.

Key Facts

  • Launch Date: April 24, 1990
  • Launch Vehicle: Space Shuttle Discovery (STS-31)
  • Mass: 11,110 kg (24,490 lb)
  • Dimensions: 13.2 m × 4.2 m (43.5 ft × 14 ft)
  • Orbit: 535 km (332 mi) altitude
  • Orbital Period: 95 minutes
  • Primary Mirror Diameter: 2.4 m (7.9 ft)
  • Servicing Missions: 5 (1993, 1997, 1999, 2002, 2009)
  • Data Collected: 150+ terabytes
  • Scientific Papers: 15,000+

Telescope Design and Instruments

The Hubble Space Telescope is a Cassegrain reflector with a 2.4-meter primary mirror. Its design allows for servicing missions to repair and upgrade its systems and scientific instruments.

Optical System

  • Primary Mirror: 2.4 m (7.9 ft) diameter
  • Focal Length: 57.6 m (189 ft)
  • Optical Design: Ritchey-Chrétien Cassegrain
  • Wavelength Range: 115-2500 nm (ultraviolet to near-infrared)
  • Angular Resolution: 0.05 arcseconds

Current Instruments

  • Wide Field Camera 3 (WFC3) - 2009
  • Cosmic Origins Spectrograph (COS) - 2009
  • Advanced Camera for Surveys (ACS) - 2002
  • Space Telescope Imaging Spectrograph (STIS) - 1997
  • Near Infrared Camera and Multi-Object Spectrometer (NICMOS) - 1997
  • Fine Guidance Sensors (FGS) - Original

Spacecraft Systems

  • Power: Two 25 ft solar arrays generating 5,500 watts
  • Communications: S-band and Ku-band antennas
  • Data Transmission: Up to 1 Mbps
  • Pointing Accuracy: 0.007 arcseconds
  • Computers: Multiple redundant systems
  • Thermal Control: Multi-layer insulation and heaters

Major Discoveries

Hubble has made numerous groundbreaking discoveries that have transformed our understanding of the universe:

Hubble Deep Field

In 1995, Hubble pointed at an apparently empty region of space for 10 days, revealing over 3,000 galaxies of various ages, shapes, and sizes, some as old as 12 billion years. This revolutionized our understanding of the early universe.

Accelerating Universe

Hubble's observations of distant supernovae helped confirm that the expansion of the universe is accelerating, leading to the theory of dark energy. This discovery was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 2011.

Exoplanet Atmospheres

Hubble made the first detection of an organic molecule in the atmosphere of an exoplanet and has continued to study exoplanet atmospheres, providing insights into worlds beyond our solar system.

Supermassive Black Holes

Hubble provided evidence that supermassive black holes exist at the centers of most galaxies, including our own Milky Way, and helped establish the relationship between black hole mass and galaxy properties.

Age of the Universe

By measuring the distances to Cepheid variable stars in distant galaxies, Hubble helped refine the Hubble constant, which determines the age of the universe, now estimated at 13.8 billion years.

Pillars of Creation

Hubble's iconic image of the Eagle Nebula's "Pillars of Creation" revealed how new stars are born within clouds of gas and dust, becoming one of the most famous astronomical images ever taken.

Servicing Missions

A unique aspect of Hubble is that it was designed to be serviced by astronauts. Five servicing missions were conducted using the Space Shuttle:

  • Servicing Mission 1 (1993): Installed corrective optics (COSTAR) to fix the spherical aberration in the primary mirror, replacing the original Wide Field/Planetary Camera with WF/PC2.
  • Servicing Mission 2 (1997): Installed two new instruments: the Space Telescope Imaging Spectrograph (STIS) and the Near Infrared Camera and Multi-Object Spectrometer (NICMOS).
  • Servicing Mission 3A (1999): Replaced all six gyroscopes, installed a new computer, and replaced a fine guidance sensor.
  • Servicing Mission 3B (2002): Installed the Advanced Camera for Surveys (ACS), which increased Hubble's discovery efficiency by a factor of 10.
  • Servicing Mission 4 (2009): The final servicing mission installed the Wide Field Camera 3 (WFC3) and the Cosmic Origins Spectrograph (COS), repaired ACS and STIS, and replaced gyroscopes, batteries, and thermal insulation.

These servicing missions not only repaired the telescope but also significantly upgraded its capabilities, allowing it to remain at the forefront of astronomical research for over three decades.

Legacy and Impact

Hubble's impact on astronomy and public perception of space has been profound:

  • Scientific Impact: Over 15,000 scientific papers have been published based on Hubble data, making it one of the most productive scientific instruments ever built.
  • Public Engagement: Hubble's stunning images have captivated the public imagination and increased interest in astronomy and space exploration.
  • Technology Development: Advances made for Hubble have benefited other space missions and Earth-based technologies, including medical imaging.
  • Educational Value: Hubble data is used in classrooms worldwide, inspiring new generations of scientists and engineers.
  • International Collaboration: As a joint project between NASA and ESA, Hubble has fostered international cooperation in space science.

Future of Hubble

While Hubble is aging, it continues to operate and make important discoveries:

  • Current projections suggest Hubble could remain operational until at least 2025, possibly longer.
  • Hubble will work in tandem with the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST), launched in 2021, with Hubble's strengths in ultraviolet and visible light complementing Webb's infrared capabilities.
  • NASA continues to monitor and maintain the telescope's systems remotely, addressing issues as they arise.
  • The scientific community continues to propose new observations and research programs, ensuring Hubble remains scientifically productive.
