Pluto

The Dwarf Planet - A World of Ice and Mystery

Overview

Pluto, once considered the ninth planet, is now classified as a dwarf planet. It's a fascinating world of ice and rock, located in the Kuiper Belt. Despite its small size, Pluto has a complex geology and atmosphere, making it one of the most interesting objects in our solar system.

Key Facts

  • Diameter: 2,377 km
  • Day Length: 6.4 Earth days
  • Distance from Sun: 5.9 billion km

Composition and Structure

Pluto is composed of about 70% rock and 30% ice. Its surface features a variety of terrains, including mountains, valleys, plains, and craters. The most notable feature is the heart-shaped Tombaugh Regio, which is made of frozen nitrogen, methane, and carbon monoxide.

Atmosphere and Weather

Pluto has a thin atmosphere composed primarily of nitrogen, with smaller amounts of methane and carbon monoxide. The atmosphere is only present when Pluto is closer to the Sun, as the gases freeze onto the surface when it's farther away. This creates a unique seasonal cycle.

Exploration History

Pluto's exploration history includes:

  • Discovered by Clyde Tombaugh in 1930
  • New Horizons (2015): First spacecraft to visit Pluto
  • Hubble Space Telescope observations
  • Ground-based telescope studies

Interesting Facts

Heart of Pluto

The heart-shaped Tombaugh Regio is one of the most distinctive features in our solar system.

Charon

Pluto's largest moon, Charon, is so large that Pluto and Charon orbit around a point between them.

Frozen Atmosphere

Pluto's atmosphere freezes and falls to the surface when it's far from the Sun.
